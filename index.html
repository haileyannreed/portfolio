LitheVision - A Data-Efficient AI Startup for Defect Detection

After assisting in computer vision research on Improving Image Segmentation using Few-Shot Learning under Professor Qian Yang in the Scientific & Computational Machine Learning Laboratory (SciMaLL), I was invited to participate in the NSF I-Corps UConn Accelerate Program as an Entrepreneurial Lead. This opportunity enabled us to explore the commercial potential of our semi-siamese neural network architecture, with the goal of transforming it into a deployable product for real-world defect detection.

LitheVision emerged from this work as a data-efficient computer vision system designed for manufacturing environments where traditional models often fail due to limited or noisy training data. Our system aims to provide a scalable, accurate, and cost-effective alternative to manual quality inspection.

Through the I-Corps program and customer discovery, we‚Äôve refined our value proposition and are actively exploring industry use cases where LitheVision can have real impact.

Stable Diffusion (CLIP + U-Net)

This project utilizes Stable Diffusion, a pipeline of pretrained modules that work together to generate images by reversing the diffusion process. It breaks down the text-to-image generation workflow into three core stages:

  • Text Embedding ‚Äì The input prompt is tokenized and transformed into embeddings using Google‚Äôs CLIP model.
  • Denoising with U-Net ‚Äì A diffuser model (U-Net) takes the CLIP embeddings and an initial latent of pure noise, and iteratively denoises it using the text guidance.
  • Image Reconstruction ‚Äì The final denoised latent is passed through a VAE decoder to generate the full-resolution image.

AI/ML Projects

Credit Card Fraud Detection

This project implements the Balanced Random Forest (BRF) algorithm to detect fraudulent transactions in the highly imbalanced Credit Card Fraud Detection dataset. By applying balanced bootstrapping and unpruned decision trees, the model improves sensitivity to minority class instances (fraud) while maintaining strong overall performance. The approach closely follows the methodology from the paper "Using Random Forest to Learn Imbalanced Data" by Chen, Liaw, and Breiman (2004), and includes evaluation with metrics like F1-score, G-Mean, and Weighted Accuracy.
