DeltaSegment

Multi-Fidelity Medical Image Segmentation via Change Detection
Overview

DeltaSegment is a computer vision framework for medical image segmentation that reframes segmentation as a change-detection problem. The project was motivated by a common challenge in biomedical imaging: obtaining large, high-quality annotated datasets is expensive and time-consuming.

Rather than relying solely on fully annotated medical images, DeltaSegment leverages paired high-fidelity and low-fidelity image representations. Low-fidelity images are generated through color quantization and paired with their original counterparts. A Semi-Siamese U-Net architecture learns differences between these representations and uses those differences as a boundary-aware signal for segmentation.

This approach enables more data-efficient learning and encourages the model to focus on structural information rather than appearance alone.

DeltaSegment was evaluated on several widely used histopathology segmentation datasets, including:

  • MoNuSeg
  • GlaS
  • TNBC

The framework demonstrated consistent improvements over strong convolutional and transformer-based segmentation baselines while maintaining strong performance in small-data settings.

The project explored:

  • Multi-fidelity learning
  • Change detection for segmentation

  • Data-efficient medical imaging models

Multi-Fidelity Medical Image Segmentation: Architectural Analysis and Optimization

UConn Honors Thesis (2026)
Overview

Following my work on DeltaSegment and multifidelity medical image segmentation, I became interested in a broader question: what is the most effective way to incorporate multifidelity information into deep learning architectures for segmentation?

This thesis extends the original DeltaSegment framework by exploring multiple architectural approaches for modeling relationships between high-fidelity and low-fidelity image representations. Rather than focusing on a single network design, the project investigates how multifidelity information can be integrated at different stages of the segmentation pipeline and evaluates the strengths and limitations of each approach.

Click to read more
Research Questions
  • How should multifidelity information be represented within a segmentation network?
  • At what stage should differences between fidelity levels be introduced?
  • Do latent-space representations outperform direct image-space differencing?
  • What limitations exist in the original DeltaSegment pipeline?
  • How well do different architectures generalize to unseen data?
Methodology

I designed and evaluated several novel architectures that incorporate multifidelity information in different ways:

  • Semi-Siamese U-Net (baseline)
  • FlexibleUNet
  • LatentDiffUNet
  • MultiDiffUNet

Models were trained and evaluated using extensive cross-validation on multiple biomedical image segmentation datasets. Hyperparameter optimization was performed using ASHA-based random search, and performance was assessed using Dice and F1 metrics.

Key Findings
  • Latent-space differencing produced the strongest overall performance.
  • Multi-scale fusion of multifidelity information improved robustness.
  • The original DeltaSegment architecture showed greater susceptibility to overfitting.
  • Standard patch-based validation procedures may overestimate real-world deployment performance.
Contributions
  • Developed three novel multifidelity segmentation architectures.
  • Conducted large-scale experimental evaluation across multiple biomedical datasets.
  • Identified limitations in existing multifidelity segmentation pipelines.
  • Established architectural guidelines for future multifidelity medical imaging research.

Quant Research: GDP Nowcasting with FRED-MD

Time-Series Modeling and Interactive Visualization for Real-Time Economic Forecasting

This project focuses on real-time GDP nowcasting using the FRED-MD panel of 126 monthly macroeconomic indicators, addressing the delay between economic activity and official GDP releases. Rather than relying solely on covariance-based statistical models, the work explores how neural network architectures can learn temporal dependencies, distinguish leading from lagging indicators, and adapt to realistic forecasting constraints.

I implemented and evaluated multiple neural forecasting models, including MLPs, CNNs, RNNs, LSTMs, and GRUs, using carefully constructed input sequences and real-time information sets that reflect data availability within each target quarter. Model performance is assessed under both stable and turbulent economic regimes and benchmarked against a naive persistence model and a Dynamic Factor Model (DFM). Results show that model effectiveness is highly regime-dependent, motivating adaptive or ensemble-based forecasting strategies rather than reliance on a single architecture.

In parallel, I designed and built an interactive Plotly Dash dashboard that integrates data preprocessing, sequence construction, and model evaluation into a unified visualization framework. The dashboard enables users to explore indicator behavior, examine leading versus lagging dynamics, and compare model predictions across economic conditions. This work was developed as part of a graduate-level Data Visualization and Communication course, and an accompanying paper documents the full pipeline, methodological choices, and insights from the visualization-driven analysis.

LitheVision

AI-Driven Visual Inspection for Manufacturing Quality Control

LitheVision is an early-stage computer vision startup developing robust, learning-based visual inspection systems for manufacturing quality control. Its core system is built on a Siamese-style deep learning architecture for change detection, enabling direct comparison between nominal (reference) and observed states while remaining resilient to environmental variation. By combining this architecture with multi-modal image analysis, LitheVision identifies subtle defects and anomalies that traditional rule-based inspection systems often miss, particularly under variable lighting, camera angles, and material conditions.

The project was developed through Accelerate UConn, the University of Connecticut’s National Science Foundation Innovation Corps (NSF I-Corps) program, where my partner and I conducted extensive customer discovery, validated market need, and refined the technical and commercial problem space through direct interviews with manufacturers and industry stakeholders.

LitheVision is currently advancing from technical validation to commercialization and is preparing a formal venture submission for the Rice Business Plan Competition, focusing on scalable deployment of AI-powered inspection tools that reduce reliance on manual inspection while improving reliability and consistency in high-stakes manufacturing environments.
